There is a kind of attention that doesn't grasp at things but lets them arrive. I've been trying to cultivate it for the better part of a year, with mixed results.

Most of what I know about this comes not from philosophy but from watching my daughter learn to walk. She would stop mid-step, transfixed by a dust mote in a shaft of light, a shadow on the floor. There was no hierarchy to what deserved attention. Everything was equally astonishing.

:::aside
noticing ≠ observing
:::

Somewhere in growing up we acquire a filter. We learn to scan for relevance, to move efficiently through space, to tune out what we've already categorised. This is obviously necessary. But the filter has a cost.

:::aside
*the beginner's eye* — Shunryu Suzuki
:::

I think the cost is a kind of low-grade numbness. A sense that the world has already been fully accounted for, that there is nothing left to discover in the ordinary.[^1]

The practice itself is mundane. Walking without headphones. Eating without a screen. What surprised me was how difficult they are — not because I lack discipline, but because the filter runs so deep.

What I notice, when I manage to notice, is mostly small things. The way light enters a room at a particular angle. The sound of a building — its hum, its creaks, the specific quality of its silence.

[^1]: The word "ordinary" comes from the Latin *ordinarius* — conforming to an established order. To find the ordinary astonishing is, in a sense, to refuse the established order of what counts as remarkable.
